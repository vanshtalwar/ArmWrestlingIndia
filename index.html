Master the Art of Armwrestling

Discover the four primary techniques used by champions worldwide: toproll, hook, press, and king's move. Each technique requires unique skill sets and offers distinct advantages depending on your physical attributes and opponent.

Explore Techniques

Toproll Technique

The toproll is one of the most effective armwrestling techniques, focusing on hand control and leverage rather than pure strength. It involves pronation (turning the palm downward) and wrist flexion to weaken the opponent's grip, forcing their fingers open and reducing their ability to apply power.

The key muscles used include the forearm flexors, brachioradialis, and pronators. Toproll is ideal for longer-armed pullers, as it maximizes leverage and prevents the opponent from utilizing their inside power effectively.

Low Toproll

Emphasizes wrist flexion while maintaining a lower hand position. Excellent for creating drag and controlling the opponent's wrist.

High Toproll

Relies more on rising and cupping movements. Creates maximum leverage and is effective against hook-style opponents.

Key Muscles

Forearm flexors, brachioradialis, pronators, and finger extensors are crucial for executing an effective toproll.

Ideal For

Pullers with longer arms, strong forearms, and those who prefer technique over raw power. Effective against stronger opponents.

Hook Technique

The hook is a power-based technique that engages the wrist, forearm, and biceps to force the opponent into an inside fight. The main goal is to curl the wrist inward, turning the match into a test of pure strength rather than hand control.

Key muscles involved are the biceps, wrist flexors, and forearm supinators. The hook is ideal for armwrestlers with strong wrists and shorter arms, as it neutralizes the opponent's hand and forces an endurance battle.

Deep Hook

Involves full wrist flexion and supination, maximizing bicep engagement for maximum power transfer.

Drag Hook

Combines hook positioning with back pressure, allowing for a controlled power application against aggressive opponents.

Key Muscles

Biceps, brachialis, wrist flexors, and forearm supinators are the primary muscle groups used in hook technique.

Ideal For

Pullers with strong biceps, thick wrists, and shorter arms. Perfect for those who excel in strength-based battles.

Press Technique

The press is an aggressive, high-risk technique that relies on body weight and triceps strength to overpower an opponent. It involves pushing the hand downward while engaging shoulder commitment and side pressure.

The move typically follows a successful wrist control or a failed toproll attempt. The press works best for armwrestlers with strong upper bodies and explosive power, but can leave the puller vulnerable if the opponent maintains hand control.

Shoulder Press

Utilizes full body engagement and shoulder commitment to apply maximum downward force on the opponent.

Triceps Press

Relies on elbow positioning and triceps strength to create a direct downward force with less body movement.

Key Muscles

Triceps, deltoids, pectorals, and trapezius muscles are heavily engaged during the execution of press techniques.

Ideal For

Pullers with strong triceps, broad shoulders, and explosive power. Works well as a follow-up move after gaining hand control.

King's Move Technique

The King's Move is a highly defensive armwrestling technique that involves extending the arm near full lock while keeping the wrist neutral or slightly supinated. This move relies on bone lock, endurance, and strategic positioning rather than pure strength.

It is most effective against stronger opponents, as it forces them into a prolonged match, tiring them out. The King's Move is controversial due to its passive nature and the extreme joint flexibility it requires, but remains legal in professional armwrestling.

Open Top Roll

A variation that uses pronation while in the extended position, providing better control against hook-style opponents.

Straight Arm Defense

Maximizes endurance by creating the most optimal bone alignment, reducing the need for muscular engagement.

Key Attributes

Joint flexibility, tendon strength, and mental endurance are more important than raw muscle power for this technique.

Ideal For

Pullers with exceptional elbow and shoulder flexibility who excel at defensive strategies against stronger opponents.
